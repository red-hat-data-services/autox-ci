## "Looks good to me": A practical guide to managing AI-generated code

**Session Code:** LT1526-CD

**Type:** Lightning talk

**Topics:** AI infrastructure and frameworks, generative AI, open source, security

### Schedule

- 02:15 PM - 02:35 PM (20 min), Room: B403 - Level 4

### Abstract

AI coding assistants like GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT, and Cursor are reshaping how we build software—and open source is no exception. These tools can now generate code, submit pull requests (PRs), and even review and merge PRs automatically—but at what cost to communities? Open source maintainers are increasingly overwhelmed by nearly correct AI-generated PRs that introduce subtle bugs and security vulnerabilities or fail to follow contributor guidelines. Many of these contributions lack transparency, traceability, and proper review, introducing a new kind of technical debt: AI entropy. And the rise of false AI-generated bug bounty reports risks adding to this overwhelm—and by undermining the spirit of responsible disclosure, potentially pushes projects to abandon bug bounty programs altogether. 

In this talk, we’ll explore a practical, security-first framework for using AI in software development and contribution workflows. We’ll review guidelines from well-respected communities—such as Linux Kernel, the Open Source Security Foundation (OpenSSF), and the Open Worldwide Application Security Project (OWASP)—and share real-world practices from industry leaders. Attendees will leave with ready-to-apply insights and resources for safely and responsibly integrating AI assistants into open source and engineering workflows, as well as using free and open source engineering-centric frameworks and tools—keeping security and trust simple and by default.

## Mitigating AI's new risk frontier: A practical guide to unifying enterprise AI security and safety

**Session Code:** LT2305-CD

**Type:** Lightning talk

**Topics:** AI infrastructure and frameworks, AI inference, security

### Schedule

- 02:40 PM - 03:00 PM (20 min), Room: B403 - Level 4

### Abstract

Enterprises are rapidly integrating AI with business data to create value, but this also expands the attack surface for adversaries. Generative AI introduces unique risks that require moving beyond patchwork defenses. Building a trustworthy AI strategy means uniting two key disciplines: AI security and AI safety. This session clarifies these domains. We’ll examine AI security, which protects the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of AI systems from threats like prompt injection and model exploitation, and contrast it with AI safety, which ensures intended and reliable behavior while preventing biased, harmful, or misleading outputs. We’ll show how Red Hat Advanced Cluster Security for Kubernetes hardens AI platforms and how TrustyAI enables transparent, explainable, and compliant AI solutions. Attendees will learn to design resilient, defense-in-depth AI architectures that address both cybersecurity and AI-specific risks. Using frameworks such as MITRE ATLAS, NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and OWASP Top 10 for large language models (LLMs), we will demonstrate how to map threats, define controls, and adopt AI responsibly. You’ll leave with a practical blueprint for building trustworthy systems that sustain business value and stakeholder confidence.

### Speakers

## Guess and check: using speculative decoding to accelerate AI inference

**Session Code:** LT2705-CD

**Type:** Lightning talk

**Topics:** AI infrastructure and frameworks, generative AI, machine learning, AI inference

### Schedule

- 04:40 PM - 05:00 PM (20 min), Room: B404 - Level 4

### Abstract

One of the fundamental bottlenecks to large language model (LLM) inference performance is the inherent requirement that LLMs generate tokens sequentially. As production model size continues to grow, this limitation poses an increasing challenge. This session will explore speculative decoding as a technique to address this problem. Speculative decoding utilizes a small "draft" model to "guess" a sequence of future tokens, allowing the main LLM to "check" multiple tokens simultaneously. When served on a platform with speculative decoding support, such as the Red Hat AI Inference Server, this can produce a significant reduction in inference time with zero degradation in accuracy. In this talk, Fynn Schmitt-Ulms and Helen Zhao will:

	Explore the theory behind speculative decoding and current algorithms. 
	Introduce the ‘speculators’ library, a new addition to the vLLM project, which enables easy creation of speculative draft models. 
	Demonstrate how to deploy a speculator model on Red Hat AI Inference Server and benchmark its performance against a traditional LLM. 
	Discuss the tradeoffs of speculative decoding in different deployment scenarios. 

By the end of the talk, you’ll understand what speculative decoding models are, how to deploy them, and when it makes sense to do so for your business needs.

  Fynn Schmitt-Ulms is a Machine Learning Engineer on the Inference Engineering ModelOpt team at Red Hat. His work focuses on speculative decoding techniques for large language models (LLMs). He is a maintainer of the open source speculators library.

### Abstract

Container platforms and AI/ML development pipelines present unique, demanding data requirements, and traditional filesystems struggle to keep up—forcing teams to rely on complex, slow, and expensive solutions. In this session, we’ll share how Btrfs, a solution that uses copy-on-write (COW) architecture, can help solve these challenges for your workloads, including:

	Containers: Discover how the native Btrfs storage driver for Podman provides superior, block-level space efficiency for image layers and supports instant container creation.
	AI/ML: Learn about instantaneous snapshots to eliminate the model checkpointing I/O tax, letting GPU-bound training to continue interruption. We’ll also cover reflinks (fast clones) for iterating multi-terabyte datasets and models in seconds.
	Data integrity: Explore how to use Btrfs features, such as support for fsverity, to provide stronger data authentication,

As Btrfs is not supported in Red Hat Enterprise Linux, we’ll also show how to adopt it for Red Hat’s platform using community builds developed by the CentOS Kernel Modules (Kmods) special interest group (SIG). You'll leave with a clear understanding of how to unlock these powerful storage capabilities for your enterprise systems.
